Welcome to the World of InfoSec

In information security, we build defenses for digital systems. Our goal is to protect information from threats. This lecture will provide a deep dive into the fundamental principles that guide us.

The Foundation: The CIA Triad

The CIA Triad is the bedrock model for security policy. It's not the Central Intelligence Agency, but a set of three core goals every secure system must achieve: Confidentiality, Integrity, and Availability.

Think of it as a three-legged stool. If any one leg is weak or missing, the entire structure becomes unstable and fails its purpose. A truly secure system must balance all three.

We'll explore each of these pillars in detail.

C is for Confidentiality

Keeping secrets secret. Also known as privacy.

Confidentiality is the principle that ensures information is not disclosed to unauthorized individuals, entities, or processes. It's about enforcing access control and protecting sensitive data from being read or seen by the wrong people.

How it's achieved:

  • Your Bank Account: Only you and authorized bank personnel can view your account balance and transaction history.
  • Medical Records: Laws like HIPAA mandate that your health information is kept private and only shared with your consent or for specific medical purposes.
  • Company Secrets: A company's new product designs are restricted to the engineering team and key executives.

Key Terms for Confidentiality

Encryption: The process of converting data into a code (ciphertext) to prevent unauthorized access. Data can be encrypted at rest (when stored on a disk) or in transit (while moving across a network).

Access Control: The selective restriction of access to a resource. This determines who is allowed to access what. Examples include usernames/passwords and file permissions (e.g., read, write, execute).

Authentication: The process of verifying the identity of a user. This can be something you know (password), something you have (phone for 2FA), or something you are (fingerprint).

Data Classification: The process of categorizing data based on its sensitivity (e.g., Public, Internal, Confidential, Restricted). This helps determine the level of protection required.

I is for Integrity

Keeping data trustworthy and accurate.

Integrity is the principle of maintaining the consistency, accuracy, and trustworthiness of data over its entire lifecycle. It ensures that data has not been subject to unauthorized modification, whether accidental or malicious.

How it's achieved:

  • File Downloads: Verifying a downloaded software installer against its published hash value ensures it wasn't tampered with to include malware.
  • Bank Transfers: When you send $100, the system must ensure the recipient gets exactly $100, not $10 or $1,000. The transaction data must not be altered.
  • Code Repositories: Using a version control system like Git ensures there is a full history of all changes to the code, preventing unauthorized or untracked modifications.

Key Terms for Integrity

Hashing: Generating a fixed-size string of characters (a hash) from a piece of data. If the data changes even slightly, the hash will change completely. Used to verify file integrity.

Digital Signatures: An electronic, encrypted stamp of authentication on digital information. It provides assurance that the message came from the claimed sender (authenticity) and has not been altered (integrity).

Checksums: Similar to a hash, a checksum is a small-sized block of data derived from another block of digital data for the purpose of detecting errors that may have been introduced during its transmission or storage.

Version Control: Systems (like Git) that track and manage changes to files over time. This allows you to recall specific versions later and ensures a clear audit trail of modifications.

A is for Availability

Ensuring access when needed.

Availability is the principle that ensures systems, applications, and data are accessible to authorized users whenever they need them. It's about ensuring uptime and resilience against service disruptions.

How it's achieved:

  • Online Shopping: A retail website must be available 24/7, especially during peak sales events like Black Friday. Downtime means lost revenue.
  • Hospital Systems: Doctors and nurses need constant access to patient records. An unavailable system could have life-threatening consequences.
  • Power Grids: The systems managing electricity distribution must be highly available to prevent blackouts.

Key Terms for Availability

Redundancy: Duplicating critical components of a system (e.g., servers, hard drives with RAID, network connections) so that if one fails, a backup can immediately take over.

Failover: The process of automatically switching to a redundant system upon the failure or abnormal termination of the previously active system.

Load Balancing: Distributing network traffic or computational workloads across multiple servers to ensure no single server becomes a bottleneck, thus improving responsiveness and availability.

Disaster Recovery: A plan for how to restore critical systems and data in the event of a major catastrophe (e.g., natural disaster, major cyberattack) that causes a complete site outage.

CIA in Practice: Online Banking

Let's see how all three apply to a single system: your online bank portal.

Confidentiality

Your password and 2FA code ensure only you can log in. Encryption protects your data from being seen by eavesdroppers on the network.

Integrity

When you transfer $50, the system ensures the amount cannot be changed to $500 in transit. Your account balance must accurately reflect all transactions.

Availability

The bank uses redundant servers and load balancers to ensure the website is online and accessible whenever you need to check your balance or pay a bill.

The Other Side: The Attacker's View

Now that we know the goals of a security professional (the "Blue Team"), let's flip the coin and look at the goals of an attacker (the "Red Team").

For every defensive principle in the CIA triad, there is a corresponding offensive goal. This is known as the DAD Triad.

The Attacker's Goal: The DAD Triad

The DAD Triad represents the attacker's objectives, which are the direct opposites of the CIA Triad. Their goal is to undermine the security posture you've built.

Disclosure (Attacks Confidentiality)

The unauthorized release of sensitive information.
Examples: Stealing and leaking customer credit card numbers, publishing private emails, a spy stealing trade secrets.

Alteration (Attacks Integrity)

The unauthorized modification of data.
Examples: Changing the amount on a digital bank transfer, defacing a website with different content, manipulating logs to hide an intrusion.

Denial (Attacks Availability)

Disrupting or preventing legitimate users from accessing a service or data.
Examples: A DDoS attack making a website crash, ransomware encrypting your files until you pay, cutting a fiber optic cable.

Lecture Summary

The CIA Triad (Defense)

  • Confidentiality: Keep it secret. (Privacy)
  • Integrity: Keep it accurate. (Trustworthiness)
  • Availability: Keep it accessible. (Uptime)

The DAD Triad (Offense)

  • Disclosure: Steal secrets.
  • Alteration: Change data.
  • Denial: Block access.

Understanding these two triads gives you a complete picture of the battlefield of information security.
